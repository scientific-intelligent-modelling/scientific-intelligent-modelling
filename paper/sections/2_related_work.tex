\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Benchmarking symbolic regression.}
The symbolic regression community has repeatedly emphasized that scientific progress is constrained by weak benchmarking discipline as much as by algorithmic limitations. SRBench \citep{lacava2021srbench} established a reproducible comparison pipeline across real-world and ground-truth problems, while recent follow-up work discusses hyperparameter tuning, execution constraints, and benchmark maintenance \citep{aldeia2025call}. Our work is aligned with this line of thinking, but addresses a different layer: rather than expanding the benchmark alone, we build the operating substrate that makes heterogeneous methods and datasets executable under a shared protocol.

\paragraph{Datasets for scientific symbolic regression.}
Dataset design has become a central issue in symbolic regression research. SRSD \citep{matsubara2022rethinking} argues that many existing datasets are poorly matched to the scientific-discovery setting and proposes more realistic formula sampling ranges together with tree-aware equation comparison. LLM-SRBench \citep{shojaee2025llmsrbench} further emphasizes split design and formula-aware evaluation for language-model-based systems. SR-Workbench builds on this perspective by standardizing multiple benchmark families into a common schema with explicit validation, ID-test, OOD-test, metadata, and optional formula verification.

\paragraph{Unified or hybrid symbolic regression frameworks.}
Several works attempt to unify symbolic regression capabilities at the algorithmic level. A representative example is the unified deep symbolic regression framework of \citet{landajuela2022udsr}, which combines multiple solution strategies within a single modular model family. Our objective is different: we unify independently maintained repositories, environments, datasets, and execution semantics across methods that were not designed to coexist in one stack.

\paragraph{Automated research infrastructures.}
Recent automated research systems have started to turn papers, repositories, and experiments into partially automated optimization loops. AutoSOTA \citep{li2026autosota}, for example, spans paper grounding, environment repair, experiment execution, ideation, and validity supervision. SR-Workbench is inspired by this infrastructure perspective but is deliberately lighter and domain-specific: it targets symbolic regression requirements such as equation outputs, complexity-sensitive evaluation, OOD reporting, and optional ground-truth validation. In this sense, it occupies the middle ground between better SR benchmarks and broader automated research systems.
